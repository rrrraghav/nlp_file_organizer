FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE 6 p.m. THURSDAY, DECEMBER 3, 1970

The cigarette industry today charged that publication this
week of two controversial papers about "smoking dogs" may
amount to “a perversion of ordinary scientific processes to promote
a public relations goal.”

Horace R. Kornegay, president of The Tobacco Institute,
called the American Cancer Society’s handling of its dog research
a “three-quarter-million-dollar fiasco,” and challenged the Society
to make public a "barrage of expert criticisms" which he said it
has received.

Earlier this year, two major medical journals refused to
publish the “smoking dog” findings. In manuscripts published
this week in Archives of Environmental Health, a specialty journal,
the Society’s researchers claim they found two “microscopic” lung
cancers after 86 beagle dogs inhaled cigarette smoke daily, forced
through holes cut in their throats, for nearly three years.

But at a heavily attended news conference in New York's
Waldorf-Astoria Hotel last February 5, the American Cancer Society
claimed there were a dozen lung cancer cases among the 86 dogs.

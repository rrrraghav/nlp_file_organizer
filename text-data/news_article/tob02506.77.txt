THE CHRISTIAN SCIENCE MONITOR

Friday, February 17, 1978

OPINION AND COMMENTARY

Richard L. Stout

Lesson from a lily pond

A few weeks ago I was sitting on the bank of a small lily pond in southern Maryland. It was one of those rare moments when time seemed to stop and everything around me became extraordinarily clear. The pond itself was unremarkable – just a muddy depression filled with water, a few lily pads, and some reeds along the edge. But it was the activity taking place on the surface that caught my attention.

Tiny insects were darting back and forth, seemingly at random, across the water’s surface. Some were struggling to stay afloat, while others appeared to be chasing each other in playful games. Occasionally, one would disappear beneath the surface, only to reappear a moment later, still frantically moving about.

I watched this scene for several minutes, mesmerized by its simplicity and beauty. Then it occurred to me that these insects were not aware of the larger world around them. They were completely absorbed in their own little dramas, oblivious to the fact that they were living in a tiny ecosystem, dependent on the pond for their survival.

This realization struck me as profoundly symbolic of human existence. We too are often so caught up in our daily routines and concerns that we fail to see the bigger picture. We become preoccupied with our individual goals and ambitions, forgetting that we are all interconnected, part of a larger whole.

The new population committee’s report on the future of food production is a stark reminder of this reality. The report warns that if current trends continue, the world will face severe food shortages within the next two decades. It points to a number of factors contributing to this crisis, including rapid population growth, environmental degradation, and unequal distribution of resources.

The committee’s recommendations are straightforward: increase agricultural productivity, reduce food waste, and promote sustainable farming practices. But implementing these changes will require a fundamental shift in our thinking – a willingness to move beyond short-term self-interest and embrace a long-term perspective.

It is easy to dismiss such warnings as alarmist or unrealistic. After all, we have always managed to overcome challenges in the past. But the scale of the problem facing us today is unprecedented. We are consuming resources at an unsustainable rate, and the consequences of our actions will be felt by generations to come.

The lily pond taught me a valuable lesson: that even the smallest creatures can teach us profound truths about life. If we are willing to take the time to observe the world around us, to appreciate its beauty and complexity, we may begin to understand our place in it – and our responsibility to protect it.

“We’re not going to solve this problem by simply saying ‘do more,’ ” said John B. Dunlop Jr., chairman of the committee. “We need a fundamental shift in how we think about food.”

The report is a sobering reminder that we are all interconnected, part of a larger ecosystem. And if we want to ensure our survival, we must begin to act like it.

Nations are benefiting from the increased productivity. The world’s farmers have been able to produce more food with fewer resources – thanks to advances in technology and improved farming techniques. But these gains are not being shared equitably. Millions of people still go hungry every day.

“The key is to make sure that the benefits of this increased productivity reach those who need them most,” said Dunlop. “We need to create a system that is both efficient and equitable.”

The committee’s report is a call to action. It is a reminder that we all have a role to play in ensuring food security for future generations. And it is a challenge to us to move beyond short-term self-interest and embrace a long-term perspective.

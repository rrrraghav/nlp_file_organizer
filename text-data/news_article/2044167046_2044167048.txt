NO INCREASE IN FEDERAL CIGARETTE TAX!

Early in 1993 the Clinton Administration sent up a trial
balloon indicating that it would seek a $2.00 increase in the
federal cigarette tax to finance its health care reform plan.
After an outpouring of angry letters and phone calls to The
White House and members of Congress, the balloon deflated.

The proposed tax was lowered first to a dollar
and ultimately to 75 cents per pack. The Administration now
seems to think that it has offered the nation’s smokers a
reasonable compromise and that a 75-cent increase is a “done
deal.”

It should think again.
Far from being an accomplished fact, the
Administration's proposal is unacceptable both to the
organized anti-smoking lobby and to supporters of smokers’
rights.

The "antis" believe the tax should be higher because
their aim is to use the tax system to price cigarettes beyond
the ability of most Americans to afford them.

But for the more than 50 million Americans who choose to
smoke, and for the hundreds of thousands of individuals whose
jobs depend -- either directly or indirectly -- on the sale
of cigarettes, any increase in the federal cigarette tax is
unacceptable.

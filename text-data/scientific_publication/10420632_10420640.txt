The general image of a highly subjected for
peptic ulcer is an inveterate coffee drinker
who smokes cigarettes and has a history of
indigestion or diarrhea (1). The data from
a recent study of former college students
support this view, but also suggest that the
risk of ulcer in later life is influenced by a
number of other factors.

The study population consisted of 20,364
male former college students who completed
a mailed questionnaire in 1974. The ques-
tionnaire elicited information on medical his-
tories, lifestyle habits, and personality traits
during the years 1920 to 1938. Histories of
peptic ulcer were recorded as well as data
on coffee consumption, soft drink consump-
tion, tobacco smoking, physical activity,
gastrointestinal symptoms, and personality
traits.

The results indicated that men who later de-
veloped peptic ulcers had, in youth, been
more likely to consume coffee and soft drinks,
to smoke cigarettes, to engage in lower levels
of physical activity, and to have a history of
indigestion or diarrhea. Certain combinations
of these characteristics were particularly asso-
ciated with an increased risk of peptic ulcer.
